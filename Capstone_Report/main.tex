\subsection*{Executive Summary}
This report details the development of an untethered spherical robot that utilizes internal electromagnets for motion and directional control along a ferromagnetic surface. Building upon a previous two-dimensional tethered prototype, the current iteration successfully integrates twenty individually controlled electromagnets within a fully enclosed spherical shell.

An onboard control system, utilizing an inertial measurement unit (IMU) and a state machine algorithm, calculates the real-time orientation of the sphere to dynamically activate the optimal electromagnets for sustained motion.

Transitioning to the 3D geometry introduced significant spatial and weight constraints. While the previous test stand relied on an external power supply, managing the onboard power requirements became a design challenge for the full sphere.

Although the 20-magnet configuration successfully demonstrates the viability of electromagnetic propulsion, system performance is currently limited by its power-to-weight ratio. Testing indicates that to achieve rapid directional changes and higher torque, future iterations will require the integration of higher-capacity batteries and an increased magnet density for a higher resolution of movement.

\section{Introduction}
Conventional motors use electrical currents, conductive windings, and magnetic materials in a variety of configurations to produce torque and control the rotation of a rotor. Switched reluctance motors (SRM) like the one shown in Figure \ref{fig:SRM_Diagram}, achieve rotation through a distribution of individually energized stator poles (electromagnets) and a simple ferromagnetic rotor. Torque is produced by sequentially energizing the different stator windings, causing the rotor to attract to the pole. This process exploits the principle of reluctance torque where the rotor naturally seeks a position which minimizes magnetic reluctance.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/SRM_Diagram.png}
    \caption[Switch Reluctance Motor Diagram]{A simplified diagram of a switched reluctance motor consists of a rotating ferromagnetic rotor, and powered stator poles.}
~\label{fig:SRM_Diagram}
\end{figure}

Our sponsor, the Project Lab, challenged our team to invert the traditional concepts of a switched reluctance motor, and design a spherical robot which uses internal electromagnets to generate controlled rolling motion along a ferromagnetic surface. Comparing the operation to an SRM, the stator becomes our robot, and the rotor becomes the ferromagnetic surface which our robot moves along. Torque and therefore motion is produced from the system’s natural tendency to minimize the reluctance (air gap) between the energized electromagnet and the ferromagnetic surface. The electromagnets inside the robot are individually controlled to manage the sphere's position and rotational speed.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}, when an individual electromagnet is powered, it generates a magnetic field (blue), which magnetizes the ferromagnetic surface, resulting in an attractive force downward. The force creates a torque, rotating the ball forward. All necessary electronics, power supplies, sensors, and control devices are contained within the body of the robot. This inverted approach allows us to apply magnetic circuit theory, develop power electronic systems, and implement advanced control algorithms in the context of a challenging and unconventional engineering problem. Concepts drawn from traditional reluctance motors aided in our design, however we did not see any projects similar to this during our research.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/Ball_torque_image2.png}
    \caption[Torque Generation of Sphere]{The electromagnets around the ball create an attractive force to the ferromagnetic surface (orange arrow), resulting in a rotation about its center (red arrow)}
    \label{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}
\end{figure}

To reduce project risk and aid in early-stage design, we focused our initial efforts on developing a simplified physical prototype: a disk that rotates on a fixed test stand above a ferromagnetic plate. This allowed us to focus on validating magnet designs, build and test the driving electronics, and prototype control software in a more manageable, two-dimensional setup. For more information on last year’s prototype, see the Project 2507 report~\cite{2507_report}.

Building on our previous work, this year we focused on system design, manufacturing, integration, and control. An optimal magnet design was chosen and twenty cores and coils were manufactured, along with twenty identical current control circuits. These isolated electromagnet subsystems were then integrated into a spherical shell geometry, along with the intertial measurement unit (IMU), microcontroller, and batteries. A control architecture was developed to use the IMU data to determine the sphere’s orientation and activate the appropriate electromagnets to maintain motion. Finally, we tested the system’s performance and identified key areas for improvement in future iterations.

\subsection{Electromagnet Theory and Core Design}
The operation of the electromagnets relies on fundamental principles of magnetic circuit theory. When current flows through the coil of an electromagnet, it produces a magnetic field that magnetizes both its ferromagnetic core and the adjacent ferromagnetic plate. This magnetization leads to the formation of two oppositely polarized magnetic regions, resulting in a strong attractive force.

The strength of this force is directly related to the magnetic flux ($\Phi$) flowing through the system. Magnetic flux is driven by magnetomotive force (MMF), analogous to voltage in electrical circuits, and opposed by magnetic reluctance ($\mathcal{R}$), which plays a role similar to electrical resistance. To decrease the reluctance and increase the flux, we can minimize the air gap between the electromagnet and the ferromagnetic plate, and use a core material with high permeability. Following detailed testing and analysis outlined in our previous report~\cite{2507_report}, we designed the magnets as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2026_magnet_design}. Noteably, the three-pronged design allows for a stronger magnetic field when it is further from the plate due to the outer ``wings'', and the central ``pole'' ensures that there is a singular location for the flux to be concentrated, which helps with stability and control. For an in-depth discussion of the design process, see section 2.2.2 of~\cite{2507_report}.

\subsection{Driving Circuitry}

\subsubsection{Theory}
The electronics in our system play a critical role in enabling control of the electromagnetic forces that drive the robot’s motion. 
The attractive force produced by a given electromagnet is directly related to the current flowing through it, so independent control of the current in each electromagnet is necessary to achieve force modulation and controlled motion. 

Electrically, the electromagnets can be modelled as a series combination of a resistance $R$ and an inductance $L$. When a voltage is applied across an electromagnet, the current through it rises with an RL exponential characteristic and when the voltage is removed, the current falls with a similar characteristic. By rapidly switching the applied voltage on and off (with MOSFET), the current can be shaped to achieve a desired average value, forming the basis of our current control strategy. Figure \ref{fig:exampleCurrent} illustrates an example electromagnet current waveform with switching driven by a PWM signal.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/exampleElectromagnetCurrent.png}
    \caption[exampleCurrent]{Illustrative example of how the current through an electromagnet is modultated through the switching of an applied voltage. When the switch is closed, the current rises with an RL exponential characteristic, and when the switch is opened, the current falls an RL exponential characteristic. The average current can be modulated by changing the duty cycle of the PWM signal, and the current ripple can be reduced by increasing the PWM switching frequency.}
    \label{fig:exampleCurrent}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} shows the high level architecture used to implement this control strategy. The switching of current through an electromagnet can give rise to large voltage spikes due to inductor kickback if not handled properly. A 'flyback' diode is used to provide a path for the electromagnet current to discharge safely when the switch is opened, and to also avoid abrupt drops in current which would otherwise degrade controllability. Furthermore, the instantaneous current through the electromagnet is monitored using a shunt resistor and differential amplifier, providing feedback for closed-loop control of the current. Based on this current measurement and the desired current, an ESP32 modulates the duty cycle of the MOSFET gate PWM signal to achieve the target current.

The high level architecture in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} can be broken down further into subsections to look at implementation specifics in more detail.

\textbf{Power}\\
Our circuit requires three distinct voltage levels to support the various subsystems. The electromagnets must be supplied by a higher voltage rail to deliver the large current necessary for actuation. According to Equation \ref{eqn:voltage_eq} a higher supply voltage also increases the rate at which current can rise through the electromagnets, improving responsiveness. As demonstrated in the ‘magnet testing section’ we found using a 24 V supply was sufficient to source currents in the 20 A range which was what we opted to rate our circuit for.  

To ensure fast switching, the gate driver is supplied with 15 V. This rail does not need to be rated for high currents.

Finally, the current sense amplifiers require dual 5 V supplies with isolated grounds. This isolation helps reduce measurement noise since the shunt is connected to a high power rail.

\textbf{Switching Stage}\\
The switching stage involves a power MOSFET (switch S1 in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic}) switching high currents at high frequencies. Power MOSFETs are standard for switching applications like this. As efficiency is not the main focus, selecting a MOSFET involves meeting a few baseline criteria. It must handle a drain-source voltage, $\text{V}_{\text{ds}}$, at least equal to the magnet supply voltage, with margin for inductive small transients. It must also support a continuous drain-source current, Ids, above the maximum current through the electromagnets, and have low on-resistance, $\text{R}_{\text{ds(on)}}$, at our switching frequencies. We used low-side switching to simplify gate driving, limiting the choice to N-channel FETs. The IRFZ44N was selected for being readily available, with a $\text{V}_{\text{ds}}$ rating of 55~V, $\text{I}_{\text{ds}}$ rating of 49~A, and suitably low $\text{R}_{\text{ds(on)}}$.

To switch the MOSFET quickly and efficiently, we placed a gate driver between the controller and the gate of each MOSFET. The gate driver provides sufficient current to charge and discharge the gate quickly so that the MOSFET spends less time in the region between being fully on and fully off, minimizing heat dissipation and losses. It also protects the microcontroller from having to source or sink more current than it is rated for. For this we used the TI UCC2742 as it provided a dual channel driver with performance that met our requirements.

The switching frequency was chosen to balance losses and current ripple. A low frequency would allow current to rise and fall significantly between PWM cycles, reducing control precision. We found 4~kHz gave good control resolution without significant heating. The MOSFET showed notable heating above 10~kHz.


\textbf{Current Sensing}\\
The shunt resistor and differential amplifier in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} make up the current sensing portion of the circuit. With the shunt placed in series with the magnet, the differential amplifier is able to monitor the voltage across the shunt, which through Ohm’s law is proportional to the current through it and the electromagnet.

\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{shunt}} = IR_{\text{shunt}}
    \label{eqn:shunt_voltage_eq}
\end{equation}

A 5 m$\Omega$ shunt was selected to minimize power loss. The result of this small resistance is a very small voltage signal, thus requiring it to be amplified. The range of current measurable is determined by the gain of the amplifier, the shunt resistance and the amplifier’s reference/supply voltage.

\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{out}} = \frac{1}{2}V_{\text{supply}}+G_{\text{amp}}IR_{\text{shunt}}
    \label{eqn:shunt_voltage_eq_w_gain}
\end{equation}

We selected the HCPL-7520 amplifier due to having good common mode voltage rejection and providing opto-isolation on the output signal. Supplying the amplifier with 5 V results in a rated gain of $\sim$9.76 V/V. For positive currents, the output of the amplifier lies between 2.5 V and 5 V. This gives a measurable current range between 0 A and 53 A which for our needs is more than enough. The output from the amplifier can be used as an input to a microcontroller where the current can be computed and closed-loop feedback control on the gate of the MOSFET can be implemented.

\textbf{Flyback and Protection}\\
As discussed in the theory section, our circuit must provide a path for current to continue to flow when switched off. This is crucial to protect circuit components from inductor kickback but also to maintain smooth and controllable current through the electromagnet. We solved this problem by placing a diode between the low side of the electromagnet and the supply as seen in Figure 18 We used the SBX3040-3G schottky diode rated for 40 V and 30 A, which meets our requirements.

\subsubsection{Orientation Tracking}
We need to know the orientation of the ball to know where each electromagnet is relative to the origin of the ball. The magnet location information allows us to determine which magnet need to be activited. 

There are a couple of popular methods for rotation tracking, 2 common approaches include Euler angles and quaternions, outlined in Figure \ref{fig:angle_tracking_options}. We chose to utlize the quaternions since it does not suffer from gimbal lock and it is more computational effiecent to compute new magnet locations (see \ref{eqn:magnet_rotation}) than the Euler angle approach.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \begin{minipage}{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/euler-angle.png}
        \caption[Euler Angle]{Euler angles is a common representation of rotation that uses three angles to represent the orientation of an object.}
    \end{minipage} 
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/quaternion-basics.png}
        \caption[Quaternion]{A quaternion is a mathematical object similar to a vector of size 4 that can represent the orientation of an object in 3D space.}
    \end{minipage}
    \label{fig:angle_tracking_options}
\end{figure}

An interntial measurment unit (IMU) is used to determine the rotation quaternion $q$. Using the Madgwick sensor fusion algorithm which combines the data from the accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer, we get a quaternion for the \cite{Madgwick_2010}. You can learn more about our selection of the IMU in \ref{sec:IMU_selection}.

Our selected IMU, provides us with current orientation "game vector" ($q$) every $100 \text{Hz}$. The game vector is sent over $I^2C$. Critically the quaternion allows us to map the coordinates of the magnets in the ball’s reference frame to the world reference frame. The arbitrary rotation of a vector $\vec{v}$ in a body with a rotational state $q$ can be calculated using the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \vec{v}_{\text{rotated}} = q \vec{v} q^{-1}
    \label{eqn:magnet_rotation}
\end{equation}

By utilizing the above formula and the known locations of the magnets in the ball's reference frame, the location of the magnets in the world reference frame is given by the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{V}_{\text{magnet, world}} &= \begin{bmatrix}
            \vec{v}_{1,\text{world}} \\
            \vdots \\
            \vec{v}_{20,\text{world}}
        \end{bmatrix}, \\
        \vec{v}_{i,\text{world}} &= q\,\vec{v}_{i, \text{ball}}\,q^{-1}, \quad i=1,\ldots,20
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eqn:magnet_world_location}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{IMU Selection}
\label{sec:IMU_selection}
There were several IMU options we considered for this project. We selected the FSM300, a 9 degree of freedom IMU with an integrated chip that preforms the Madgwick sensor fusion on device. 

Our IMU performance was analyzed using a stable drift test. In this test, we placed the IMU on a stable surface and recorded the output quaternion over 5 minutes. We found that the FSM300 had a $10^5$ times lower drift than the BN09X, a cheaper alternative.  

\subsubsection{Magnet Selection}
\label{sec:magnet_selection}
To control the rolling motion of the sphere, the system must continuously generate a desired force vector in the global reference frame ($\vec{F}_{\text{world}}$). The user's joystick input dictates a desired two-dimensional velocity. A Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller compares this input against the sphere's current estimated velocity to calculate the kinematic error, $\vec{e}(t) = \vec{v}_{\text{desired}} - \vec{v}_{\text{current}}$. 

By minimizing this velocity error, the controller outputs the required global corrective force:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{F}_{\text{world}} = K_p \vec{e}(t) + K_i \int_{0}^{t} \vec{e}(\tau) d\tau + K_d \frac{d\vec{e}(t)}{dt}
\end{equation}

For each magnet, the algorithm evaluates its angle from the steel plate to find its expected torque from a lookup table based on data from the test stand. To find the optimal combination of magnets to match the desired force, the system evaluates both individual candidates and pairs by minimizing a cost function. This function balances the target force error against an electrical current ($I_j$) penalty ($\lambda$):
\begin{equation}
    \text{Score} = \left\| \vec{F}_{\text{body}} - \sum_{j} (\vec{v}_j I_j) \right\| + \lambda \sum_{j} I_j
\end{equation}

The combination (single or pair) that yields the lowest score is selected for activation. Finally, all commanded currents are capped at the current limit. Capping the magnets individually rather than scaling all selected magnets down by the same ratio ensures strength is as high as possible at the cost of changing the angle of the resulting force vector. This tradeoff was made to prioritize responsiveness and agility over precision in the direction of the force vector.

\subsubsection{Optimization of Coil Winding Count}
Selecting the ideal wire loop count ($N$) requires balancing magnetic strength against the rotational inertia added by the wire's mass. Because the coil's diameter expands with each loop, inertia scales non-linearly. To maximize the robot's angular acceleration, we optimized the strength-to-inertia ratio subject to a constant thermal limit ($I^2R$). 

Our mathematical model revealed an optimal configuration at $N=202$ loops. See Appendix \ref{sec:coil_optimization} for a detailed derivation of the ideal loop count calculations.

\subsubsection{Active State}
\label{sec:active}
In the active state, our ESP runs 2 separate processes, one on each core on the esp. One core is reasonable for receiving and sending data to the dashboard. The other core is responsible for the orientation tracking, magnet selection, and magnet current control. 

The two cores communicate through a globally shared object that contains the current state of the system. All shared memory is protected by mutexes to prevent race conditions between cores. More detail on the shared data between cores can be found in (appendix TODO: add section on shared data).

The control loop is run on core 1 with the watchdog turned off to allow us to run our fast loop at $3.33 \text{KHz}$. Every 10 milliseconds, the algorithm checks for new orientation data from the IMU. The magnet selection algorithm runs using the new locations and the most recent joystick command. After 2 magnets and their current targets are updated, the logic returns to the fast current loop is run with the updated current targets. 

The communication loop on core 0 is responsible for receiving data from the dashboard and sending data back to the dashboard. The outbound data is sent at a rate of $10 \text{Hz}$ and contains the following state variables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current orientation of the ball (quaternion)
    \item Current target for each magnet
    \item Current (and timestamp) through each magnet
\end{itemize}
The inbound data from the dashboard uses a polling approach. When a new direction command is received from the dashboard, the communication loop updates the global state with the new command.




\subsubsection{Calibration State}
\label{sec:calibration}
The connection between the direction of the joystick and the local axis of the ball is challenging to connect. Our calibration step is designed to help align the pair of coordinate systems, and is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The magnet with the largest force vector is selected and turned on at $12 \text{amps}$ for 1 second.
    \item The user draws the joystick in the direction of travel of the ball and send that direction to the ball.
    \item The ball sets the yaw offset between the magnet and the joystick direction. The offset is then applied to all future joystick commands.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Time Constant}\\
The electrical time constant of the circuit is an important parameter to consider. To experimentally determine this value, we used our current controller to set a non-zero target current, then a zero target, and recorded the waveform as it decayed.

We performed this test in two configurations: placing the magnet directly above the ferromagnetic plate, and in the free air away from the plate. Placing the magnet near the plate effectively increases the coil’s inductance and thus the time constant $\tau$. This test was conducted to evaluate how significant this change would affect the response time and whether it could impact control performance. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:time_constant}, the measured time constant in free air was 5.82 ms, while the time constant above the plate increased to 7.34 ms. This difference aligns with expectations given the inductance change, but in both cases, the values remain quite small. We expect that the system’s mechanical inertia will dominate, and the electrical time constant will not pose a limitation on controls.

\subsubsection{Icosahedral Geometry and Split Plane Design}
Maintaining spherical symmetry in our design was critical. Rotational inertia, available torque generation, and control complexity are simplified when the system behaves the same in all orientations. From our previous work, and magnet design testing, we found that the magnets were only effective when they were rotated to within 45 degrees of the ferromagnetic plate. These requirements led us to a icosahedral geometry which has twenty faces that are equally spaced around the surface, with 42 degree angles between magnet centers. This allows us to place one magnet at the center of each face. We designed the sphere to be split into two hemispheres along a edge of the icosahedron, ensuring that no magnets are located on the split plane. This design choice allows for access to the internal components without the need to remove any magnets. See figure~\ref{fig:split_plane} for an image of the split plane design and how the two halves of the sphere combine.

\subsubsection{Integration}
To connect the current driver boards to the microcontroller and batteries, four PCBs were designed and built, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:integration}. The two power distribution boards provide the battery power to the twenty current driver boards, while the signal distribution board takes the microcontroller’s control signals and routes them to the current driver boards. The main microcontroller board had three eight-channel analog to digital converters (ADCs) which read the current sense amplifier outputs and multiplex the signals to send to the microcontroller. Additionally, two sixteen-channel PWM drivers control the gate drivers, with ten magnets per driver. The ADCs use SPI protocol and scan all of the input signals, while the PWM drivers used the I2C protocol to communicate with the microcontroller and send individual PWM signals to each magnet. The microcontroller board also connected to an IMU board using I2C. Each half of the sphere contains ten magnets, and a power distribution board. The signal distribution board pairs with a smaller board in the second half to route the signals to those driver boards.

\section{Conclusion}
The primary objective of this project was to develop a three-dimensional spherical robot, capable of moving using the forces generated by internal electromagnets. By successfully integrating twenty individually controlled electromagnets, with a battery supply, IMU and microcontroller, we have proven that internal electromagnetic propulsion is a viable method for achieving motion along a ferromagnetic surface. The successful implementation of the state machine algorithm demonstrates that the system can perform the complex, real-time orientation calculations required to activate the correct electromagnets for sustained movement.

While this prototype successfully validates the core mechanical, electrical, and software architecture, the current design faces performance bottlenecks primarily related to weak torque generation. This weakness stems from several distinct operational and design limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power Delivery:} Operationally, the current LiPo batteries experience voltage drop during use and struggle to consistently deliver the 20 amps of instantaneous current required for optimal performance.
    \item \textbf{Control Algorithm Limitations:} The current magnet activation algorithm relies on a simple, greedy approach which selects the two magnets delivering the most force in the desired direction. This fails to properly handle the ball's rotational inertia and does not optimize for energy efficiency or advantageous future orientations.
    \item \textbf{Material Weight:} The weight-to-magnetic-force ratio of the current magnet materials significantly increases the system's polar moment of inertia, mechanically restricting acceleration.
    \item \textbf{Angular Resolution:} Design-wise, utilizing only 20 magnets limits our angular resolution, leaving large gaps of approximately $30^\circ$ between vertical axes. This often forces the robot to travel in suboptimal directions when no appropriately aligned magnets are available.

\end{itemize}

\section{Recommendations}
Our final design shows a promising path forward for a spherical robot propelled by electromagnets. To address the bottlenecks identified in the conclusion and significantly enhance performance, we recommend the following actionable developments for future iterations or capstone projects:

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{1. Integrate Higher Capacity Batteries}\\
Source and install batteries with higher overall capacity and faster instantaneous discharge capabilities to reliably sustain 20 amps of current.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{2. Implement Advanced Magnet Activation Algorithms}\\
Upgrade the magnet selection logic to handle complex motion planning. Specific avenues to explore include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A cost function optimized to place the ball in future orientations that maximize the number of usable magnets.
    \item A cost function optimized for the most efficient use of electrical current.
    \item An improved inertia correction model that dynamically accounts for the ball's current momentum.
    \item A reinforcement learning algorithm that allows the system to independently learn the optimal magnet selection strategy.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{3. Increase the Number of Magnets}\\
Scale the internal design to incorporate 32 or more electromagnets. This will improve angular resolution, eliminate large dead zones, and ensure stronger torque generation in arbitrary directions.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{4. Utilize Improved Magnet Materials}\\
Transition to magnet materials with a superior weight-to-magnetic-force ratio. This will maximize magnetic strength while actively reducing the system's polar moment of inertia.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{5. Explore Sheetless Propulsion Methods}\\
Investigate entirely new design families that eliminate the need for a ferrous operating surface. One recommended approach is integrating an outer spherical shell filled with ferromagnetic fluid to generate motion by dynamically shifting the system's center of mass rather than relying on external magnetic flux.

\section{Derivation of Optimal Coil Winding Count}
\label{sec:coil_optimization}

To maximize the angular acceleration of the spherical robot, we must maximize the ratio of magnetic torque to total rotational inertia. Changing the number of wire loops ($N$) alters the magnetic strength, the electrical resistance (and thus thermal limits), and the mass of the coils.

\subsection*{1. Geometric Mass Model}
The total mass of the wire scales non-linearly with $N$ because the coil's diameter expands with each wrap, increasing the length of wire required per loop. Based on the physical prototype, the coil has an inner diameter $D_{\text{inner}} = 16$~mm. At $N=250$, the outer diameter is $D_{\text{outer}} = 42$~mm. 

Approximating the coil growth as a continuous sheet, the thickness added per loop is $t = (42 - 16) / (2 \cdot 250) = 0.052$~mm. The average diameter at any loop count is $D_{\text{avg}} = (D_{\text{inner}} + D_{\text{outer}}) / 2$. Because the wire's total weight $W(N)$ is proportional to $N \cdot D_{\text{avg}}$, we establish the function:
\begin{equation}
    W(N) \propto N(0.052N + 16)
\end{equation}

\subsection*{2. Rotational Inertia Model}
The rotational inertia of the sphere is the sum of the base sphere's inertia ($J_{\text{base}}$) and the inertia contributed by the eight electromagnets ($J_{\text{coils}}$). 

Our initial prototype ($N=250$) had an estimated total inertia of $0.033 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$. The twenty 100~g coils contributed approximately $0.00895 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$ to this total, allowing us to isolate the base inertia:
$$J_{\text{base}} = 0.033 - 0.00895 = 0.02405 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$

Since the coil inertia scales linearly with coil mass, we use the geometric mass model to define the total rotational inertia as a function of $N$:
\begin{equation}
    J(N) = 0.02405 + 1.236 \times 10^{-6} \cdot N(0.052N + 16)
\end{equation}

\subsection*{3. Thermal Constraint and Magnetic Strength}
To prevent overheating, the power dissipated as heat ($P = I^2R$) must remain constant. The electrical resistance $R$ is directly proportional to the total length of the wire, meaning $R(N) \propto W(N)$. To maintain a constant heat output, the allowable current $I(N)$ must be adjusted according to:
\begin{equation}
    I_1^2 R_1 = I_2^2 R_2 \implies I(N) = I_{250} \sqrt{\frac{W(250)}{W(N)}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{N(0.052N + 16)}}
\end{equation}

Magnetic strength ($S$) scales linearly with Amp-turns ($NI$). Substituting the thermally constrained current yields a strength function that increases with $N$, but with diminishing returns due to the expanding resistance:
\begin{equation}
    S(N) = N \cdot I(N) \propto \frac{N}{\sqrt{N(0.052N + 16)}}
\end{equation}

\subsection*{4. Optimization}
Angular acceleration ($\alpha$) is proportional to $\frac{\text{Torque}}{\text{Inertia}}$. Assuming torque scales linearly with magnetic strength (which in our region of operation is a valid assumption), we define our objective function to maximize:
\begin{equation}
    f(N) = \frac{S(N)}{J(N)} = \frac{N}{\sqrt{N(0.052N + 16)} \left( 0.02405 + 1.236 \times 10^{-6} \cdot N(0.052N + 16) \right)}
\end{equation}

Evaluating the derivative $f'(N) = 0$ identifies a global maximum at $N = 202$ loops. At this optimal configuration, the system achieves the highest theoretical acceleration while strictly adhering to the thermal dissipation limits of the original 250-loop design.
