\subsection*{Executive Summary}
This report details the development of an untethered spherical robot that utilizes internal electromagnets for propulsion and directional control along a steel surface. Building upon a previous two-dimensional tethered prototype, the current iteration successfully integrates twenty individually controlled electromagnets within a fully enclosed spherical shell.

An onboard control system, utilizing an inertial measurement unit (IMU) and a state machine algorithm, calculates the real-time orientation of the sphere to dynamically activate the optimal electromagnets for sustained motion.

Transitioning to the 3D geometry introduced significant spatial and weight constraints. While the previous test stand relied on an external power supply, managing the onboard power requirements became a design challenge for the full sphere.

Although the 20-magnet configuration successfully demonstrates the viability of electromagnetic propulsion, system performance is currently limited by its power-to-weight ratio. Testing indicates that to achieve rapid directional changes and higher torque, future iterations will require the integration of higher-capacity batteries and an increased magnet density for a higher resolution of movement.

\section{Introduction}
Conventional motors use electrical currents, conductive windings, and magnetic materials in a variety of configurations to produce torque and control the rotation of a rotor. Switched reluctance motors (SRM) like the one shown in Figure \ref{fig:SRM_Diagram}, achieve rotation through a distribution of individually energized stator poles (electromagnets) and a simple ferromagnetic rotor. Torque is produced by sequentially energizing the different stator windings, causing the rotor to attract to the pole. This process exploits the principle of reluctance torque where the rotor naturally seeks a position which minimizes magnetic reluctance.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/SRM_Diagram.png}
    \caption[Switch Reluctance Motor Diagram]{A simplified diagram of a switched reluctance motor consists of a rotating ferromagnetic rotor, and powered stator poles.}
    \label{fig:SRM_Diagram}
\end{figure}

Our sponsor, the Project Lab, challenged our team to invert the traditional concepts of a switched reluctance motor, and design a spherical robot which uses internal electromagnets to generate controlled rolling motion along a ferromagnetic surface. Comparing the operation to an SRM, the stator becomes our robot, and the rotor becomes the ferromagnetic surface which our robot moves along. Torque and therefore motion is produced from the system’s natural tendency to minimize the reluctance (air gap) between the energized electromagnet and the ferromagnetic surface. The electromagnets inside the robot are individually controlled to manage the sphere's position and rotational speed.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}, when an individual electromagnet is powered, it generates a magnetic field (blue), which magnetizes the ferromagnetic surface, resulting in an attractive force downward. The force creates a torque, rotating the ball forward. All necessary electronics, power supplies, sensors, and control devices are contained within the body of the robot. This inverted approach allows us to apply magnetic circuit theory, develop power electronic systems, and implement advanced control algorithms in the context of a challenging and unconventional engineering problem. Concepts drawn from traditional reluctance motors aided in our design, however we did not see any projects similar to this during our research.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/Ball_torque_image2.png}
    \caption[Torque Generation of Sphere]{The electromagnets around the ball create an attractive force to the ferromagnetic surface (orange arrow), resulting in a rotation about its center (red arrow)}
    \label{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}
\end{figure}

To reduce project risk and aid in early-stage design, we focused our initial efforts on developing a simplified physical prototype: a disk that rotates on a fixed test stand above a ferromagnetic plate. This allowed us to focus on validating magnet designs, build and test the driving electronics, and prototype control software in a more manageable, two-dimensional setup. For more information on last year’s prototype, see the Project 2507 report~\cite{2507_report}.

Building on our previous work, this year we focused on system design, manufacturing, integration, and control. An optimal magnet design was chosen and twenty cores and coils were manufactured, along with twenty identical current control circuits. These isolated electromagnet subsystems were then integrated into a spherical shell geometry, along with the intertial measurement unit (IMU), microcontroller, and batteries. A control architecture was developed to use the IMU data to determine the sphere’s orientation and activate the appropriate electromagnets to maintain motion. Finally, we tested the system’s performance and identified key areas for improvement in future iterations.

\subsection{Electromagnet}
\subsubsection{Theory}

The operation of the electromagnets relies on fundamental principles of magnetic circuit theory. When current flows through the coil of an electromagnet, it produces a magnetic field that magnetizes both its ferromagnetic core and the adjacent steel plate. This magnetization leads to the formation of two oppositely polarized magnetic regions, resulting in a strong attractive force.

The strength of this force is directly related to the magnetic flux ($\Phi$) flowing through the system. Magnetic flux is driven by magnetomotive force (MMF), analogous to voltage in electrical circuits, and opposed by magnetic reluctance ($\mathcal{R}$), which plays a role similar to electrical resistance. To decrease the reluctance and increase the flux, we can minimize the air gap between the electromagnet and the steel plate, and use a core material with high permeability. Following detailed testing and analysis outlined in our previous report~\cite{2507_report}, we designed the magnets as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2026_magnet_design}. Noteably, the three-pronged design allows for a stronger magnetic field when it is further from the plate due to the outer ``wings'', and the central ``pole'' ensures that there is a singular location for the flux to be concentrated, which helps with stability and control. For an in-depth discussion of the design process, see section 2.2.2 of~\cite{2507_report}.

\subsubsection{Saturation}
To evaluate the performance limits of our trident magnet, we tested for magnetic flux saturation. Once saturation occurs, increasing current no longer results in greater flux and torque, making it essential to determine whether our design operated within the linear region of the material’s magnetic saturation curve. If saturation occurs, then any additional power added to the magnets is wasted. 

We compared two trident cores—12 mm and 16 mm thick—each tested at a 20$^\circ$ angle relative to the steel plate. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:Saturation}, the 12 mm core saturated at approximately 1200 Amp-Turns (6.8 A), while the 16 mm version showed no signs of saturation up to 3000 Amp-Turns (14 A), our test limit. Given that the thicker core did not saturate at 14 A, we opted to design our driving circuit to be rated for up to 20 A. In doing so, we can use more of the available torque from the magnet.

Beyond avoiding saturation, the 16 mm core also produced higher torque across all tested angles, indicating that increased thickness not only extends the linear operating range but improves overall performance. For our spherical design, we aim to operate just below the saturation threshold to maximize torque efficiency without adding unnecessary material and weight.

\subsection{Driving Circuitry}

\subsubsection{Theory}
The electronics in our system play a critical role in enabling control of the electromagnetic forces that drive the robot’s motion. Because the attractive force produced by each electromagnet is directly related to the current flowing through it, we can control the motion of the disk by modulating these currents. At a high level, the requirements for the circuit behaviour are simple: predictably deliver and regulate high currents to the electromagnets. 

Circuits used in current control applications utilize special properties of inductors. An inductor’s ability to resist sudden changes in current make them naturally suited for use in smoothing or shaping current. Conveniently, the electromagnets in our system act as inductors themselves. Each electromagnet in the system can be modeled as a series combination of a resistance $R$ and an inductance $L$. When a constant voltage is applied across the electromagnet, the instantaneous current through it is governed by the differential equation:

\begin{equation}
V = L \frac{dI}{dt} + RI
\label{eqn:voltage}
\end{equation}

The solutions to Equation \ref{eqn:voltage} describe the behaviour of current in both the charging and discharging cases:

\begin{equation}
    I(t) = V_0 R \left( 1 - e^{-\frac{R}{L}t} \right) \quad \text{for} \quad V = 0
    \label{eqn:current}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    I(t) = I_0 e^{-\frac{R}{L}t} \quad \text{for} \quad V \neq0
    \label{eqn:current_zero}
\end{equation}


When the applied voltage is non-zero, the current through the electromagnet rises according to Equation \ref{eqn:current}. Disconnecting the source sets $V = 0$, and the current decreases according to Equation \ref{eqn:current_zero}. In both cases the current exhibits an exponential characteristic governed by the time constant $\tau = L/R$, where larger $\tau$ corresponds to slower changes in current. The time constant of the electromagnet is important to consider in the design of our driving circuit as it defines how quickly the electromagnet can generate or remove a force. Understanding this behaviour when implementing the control software is important as it allows us to account for current lag and ensure magnetic forces are applied when needed. If we know the time constant of the electromagnet we can shift the timing of inputs slightly so the electromagnet current and force reaches target levels by the time it is required.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{Circuit Visuals/PWM Image.png}
    \caption[PWM Control Signal]{Example of electromagnet current control}
    \label{fig:PWM}
\end{figure}

As seen in Figure \ref{fig:PWM}, by using pulse width modulation (PWM), we can switch the voltage applied to the electromagnet on and off at high frequencies and chain together the charging and discharging characteristics of the current. As a result, the effective current through the electromagnet is the average of this repeated waveform. By changing the duty cycle of the PWM signal we can control the average current value over time.

An additional phenomenon that arises when dealing with inductors, and something our circuit must be designed to handle safely is inductor kickback. Kickback is best understood through the voltage-current relationship for an ideal inductor.

\begin{equation}
    V = L \frac{d I}{d t}
    \label{eqn:voltage_eq}
\end{equation}

Equation \ref{eqn:voltage_eq} says that the voltage across an inductor is proportional to the rate of change of current through it. As discussed earlier, our system will control current by rapidly switching the applied voltage on and off, which effectively involves connecting and disconnecting the path between the power source, electromagnet, and ground. However, if current is flowing through the electromagnet and we suddenly break the circuit, Equation \ref{eqn:voltage_eq} says the rapid drop in current causes the inductor to generate a very large voltage spike in an attempt to keep current flowing. To avoid this, our circuit must provide a path for current to discharge when the switch is opened. Otherwise, we risk damaging circuit components.

\subsubsection{Design}
Equipped with an understanding of the circuit’s requirements, and the underlying physical phenomena, we can put together the high-level architecture of the driving circuit to be used by each electromagnet. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{Circuit Visuals/Circuit Schematic.png}
    \caption[Schematic]{High level driving circuit schematic}
    \label{fig:Schematic}
\end{figure}

The high level architecture in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} can be broken down further into subsections to look at implementation specifics in more detail.

\textbf{Power}\\
Our circuit requires three distinct voltage levels to support the various subsystems. The electromagnets must be supplied by a higher voltage rail to deliver the large current necessary for actuation. According to Equation \ref{eqn:voltage_eq} a higher supply voltage also increases the rate at which current can rise through the electromagnets, improving responsiveness. As demonstrated in the ‘magnet testing section’ we found using a 24 V supply was sufficient to source currents in the 20 A range which was what we opted to rate our circuit for.  

To ensure fast switching, the gate driver is supplied with 15 V. This rail does not need to be rated for high currents.

Finally, the current sense amplifiers require dual 5 V supplies with isolated grounds. This isolation helps reduce measurement noise since the shunt is connected to a high power rail.

\textbf{Switching Stage}\\
The switching stage involves a power MOSFET (switch S1 in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic}) switching high currents at high frequencies. Power MOSFETs are standard for switching applications like this. As efficiency is not the main focus, selecting a MOSFET involves meeting a few baseline criteria. It must handle a drain-source voltage, $\text{V}_{\text{ds}}$, at least equal to the magnet supply voltage, with margin for inductive small transients. It must also support a continuous drain-source current, Ids, above the maximum current through the electromagnets, and have low on-resistance, $\text{R}_{\text{ds(on)}}$, at our switching frequencies. We used low-side switching to simplify gate driving, limiting the choice to N-channel FETs. The IRFZ44N was selected for being readily available, with a $\text{V}_{\text{ds}}$ rating of 55~V, $\text{I}_{\text{ds}}$ rating of 49~A, and suitably low $\text{R}_{\text{ds(on)}}$.

To switch the MOSFET quickly and efficiently, we placed a gate driver between the controller and the gate of each MOSFET. The gate driver provides sufficient current to charge and discharge the gate quickly so that the MOSFET spends less time in the region between being fully on and fully off, minimizing heat dissipation and losses. It also protects the microcontroller from having to source or sink more current than it is rated for. For this we used the TI UCC2742 as it provided a dual channel driver with performance that met our requirements.

The switching frequency was chosen to balance losses and current ripple. A low frequency would allow current to rise and fall significantly between PWM cycles, reducing control precision. We found 4~kHz gave good control resolution without significant heating. The MOSFET showed notable heating above 10~kHz.


\textbf{Current Sensing}\\
The shunt resistor and differential amplifier in Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} make up the current sensing portion of the circuit. With the shunt placed in series with the magnet, the differential amplifier is able to monitor the voltage across the shunt, which through Ohm’s law is proportional to the current through it and the electromagnet.

\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{shunt}} = IR_{\text{shunt}}
    \label{eqn:shunt_voltage_eq}
\end{equation}

A 5 m$\Omega$ shunt was selected to minimize power loss. The result of this small resistance is a very small voltage signal, thus requiring it to be amplified. The range of current measurable is determined by the gain of the amplifier, the shunt resistance and the amplifier’s reference/supply voltage.

\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{out}} = \frac{1}{2}V_{\text{supply}}+G_{\text{amp}}IR_{\text{shunt}}
    \label{eqn:shunt_voltage_eq_w_gain}
\end{equation}

We selected the HCPL-7520 amplifier due to having good common mode voltage rejection and providing opto-isolation on the output signal. Supplying the amplifier with 5 V results in a rated gain of $\sim$9.76 V/V. For positive currents, the output of the amplifier lies between 2.5 V and 5 V. This gives a measurable current range between 0 A and 53 A which for our needs is more than enough. The output from the amplifier can be used as an input to a microcontroller where the current can be computed and closed-loop feedback control on the gate of the MOSFET can be implemented.

\textbf{Flyback and Protection}\\
As discussed in the theory section, our circuit must provide a path for current to continue to flow when switched off. This is crucial to protect circuit components from inductor kickback but also to maintain smooth and controllable current through the electromagnet. We solved this problem by placing a diode between the low side of the electromagnet and the supply as seen in Figure 18 We used the SBX3040-3G schottky diode rated for 40 V and 30 A, which meets our requirements.

\end{figure}

An interntial measurment unit (IMU) can be used to update the rotation quaternion $q$ in real time. The quaternion can be calculated by using a famous Madgwick sensor fusion algorithm which combines the data from the accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer outputting a quaternion representing the current state of the ball \cite{Madgwick_2010}. You can learn more about our selection of the IMU in \ref{sec:IMU_selection}

With the quaternion representing the current orientation of the ball, we can determine the location of the magnets relative to the origin of the ball. Critically using the quaternion allows us to map the coordinates of the magnets in the ball’s reference frame to the world reference frame, which is critical allows to align the magnet locations with the joystick input. The rotation of a vector $\vec{v}$ by a quaternion $q$ can be calculated using the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \vec{v}_{\text{rotated}} = q \vec{v} q^{-1}
    \label{eqn:magnet_rotation}
\end{equation}

By utilizing the above formula and the known locations of the magnets in the ball's reference frame we are able to determine the location of each magnet in the world reference frame. 

\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{V}_{\text{magnet, world}} &= \begin{bmatrix}
            \vec{v}_{1,\text{world}} \\
            \vdots \\
            \vec{v}_{20,\text{world}}
        \end{bmatrix}, \\
        \vec{v}_{i,\text{world}} &= q\,\vec{v}_{i, \text{ball}}\,q^{-1}, \quad i=1,\ldots,20
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eqn:magnet_world_location}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{IMU Selection}
\label{sec:IMU_selection}
There were severel IMU options we considered for this project. We ultimetely selected the FSM300, a 9 degree of freedom IMU with an integrated chip that preforms the Madgwick sensor fusion on device. 

Our IMU selection was determined using a stable drift test. In this test, we placed the IMU on a stable surface and recorded the output quaternion over 5 minutes. We found that the FSM300 had a $10^5$ times lower drift than the BN09X (the cheaper alternative).

\textbf{Time Constant}\\
The electrical time constant of the circuit is an important parameter to consider. To experimentally determine this value, we used our current controller to set a non-zero target current, then a zero target, and recorded the waveform as it decayed.

We performed this test in two configurations: placing the magnet directly above the steel plate, and in the free air away from the plate. Placing the magnet near the plate effectively increases the coil’s inductance and thus the time constant $\tau$. This test was conducted to evaluate how significant this change would affect the response time and whether it could impact control performance. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:time_constant}, the measured time constant in free air was 5.82 ms, while the time constant above the plate increased to 7.34 ms. This difference aligns with expectations given the inductance change, but in both cases, the values remain quite small. We expect that the system’s mechanical inertia will dominate, and the electrical time constant will not pose a limitation on controls.

\section{Conclusion}
This project explored whether electromagnets alone could propel and control a spherical robot on a ferromagnetic surface. Our goal was to demonstrate feasibility and understand how core design, magnetic force generation, and torque control relate to current input.

We confirmed that torque scales with Amp-Turns, meaning wire gauge and winding count are less critical than core geometry, and saturation.

The 16 mm trident core was the strongest, showing no saturation up to 3000 Amp-Turns and producing significant torque across all angles. These results guide future designs: operating just below saturation should optimize performance while minimizing weight.

Our disk prototype replicates core dynamics and enables control testing with a rotary encoder. However, transitioning to full spherical motion introduces new control and stability challenges, particularly in mapping magnet orientation relative to an IMU.

Based on our successful demonstration, continued investment in this project is justified and will yield a fully operational spherical robot.

\section{Recommendations}
The transition from the disk to a spherical robot introduces substantial design complexity, particularly in mechanical integration, control, and power systems. To support this next stage of development, we recommend the following key actions:

\textbf{Develop a 3D-Symmetric Electromagnet Design}\\
The trident geometry was optimized for planar motion in the disk prototype. For spherical implementation, the electromagnet design should be revised for three-dimensional symmetry to ensure consistent torque generation in all orientations. A proposed 3D design is included in Figure \ref{fig:three_images} (Appendix \ref{Spherical Images Appendix}).

\textbf{Integrate Optocoupleers and Redesign Driving PCBs}\\
To protect microcontroller signals from high-power switching noise, optical isolation should be integrated into future PCB designs. A PCB will improve reliability and simplify assembly.

\textbf{Design Modular Spherical Housing}\\
Each electromagnet functions as an independent subsystem. We recommend designing a modular spherical housing with magnets arranged in an icosahedral geometry (20 faces), which has uniform coverage and ease of assembly or replacement.

\textbf{Investigate Power Supply Instability}\\
Although motion control currently performs as expected, the power supplies exhibit unstable behavior. Diagnosing and resolving this issue—potentially caused by inrush currents, back EMF, or insufficient filtering—will be critical for safe and reliable operation.

\textbf{Model Electromagnet Positions and Orientations}\\
 Developing a 3D model of magnet positions and orientations will support integration with onboard IMUs and improve closed-loop control in three-dimensional motion.
