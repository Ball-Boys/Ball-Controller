\subsection*{Executive Summary}
This report details the development of an untethered spherical robot that utilizes internal electromagnets for motion and directional control along a ferromagnetic surface. Building upon a previous two-dimensional tethered prototype, the current iteration successfully integrates twenty individually controlled electromagnets within a fully enclosed spherical shell.

An onboard control system, utilizing an inertial measurement unit (IMU) and a state machine algorithm, calculates the real-time orientation of the sphere to dynamically activate the optimal electromagnets for sustained motion.

Transitioning to the 3D geometry introduced significant spatial and weight constraints. While the previous test stand relied on an external power supply, managing the onboard power requirements became a design challenge for the full sphere.

Although the 20-magnet configuration successfully demonstrates the viability of electromagnetic propulsion, system performance is currently limited by its power-to-weight ratio. Testing indicates that to achieve rapid directional changes and higher torque, future iterations will require the integration of higher-capacity batteries and an increased magnet density for a higher resolution of movement.

\section{Introduction}
Conventional motors use electrical currents, conductive windings, and magnetic materials in a variety of configurations to produce torque and control the rotation of a rotor. Switched reluctance motors (SRM) like the one shown in Figure \ref{fig:SRM_Diagram}, achieve rotation through a distribution of individually energized stator poles (electromagnets) and a simple ferromagnetic rotor. Torque is produced by sequentially energizing the different stator windings, causing the rotor to attract to the pole. This process exploits the principle of reluctance torque where the rotor naturally seeks a position which minimizes magnetic reluctance.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/SRM_Diagram.png}
    \caption[Switched reluctance motor diagram]{A simplified diagram of a switched reluctance motor consists of a rotating ferromagnetic rotor, and powered stator poles.}\label{fig:SRM_Diagram}
\end{figure}

Our sponsor, the Project Lab, challenged our team to invert the traditional concepts of a switched reluctance motor, and design a spherical robot which uses internal electromagnets to generate controlled rolling motion along a ferromagnetic surface. Comparing the operation to an SRM, the stator becomes our robot, and the rotor becomes the ferromagnetic surface which our robot moves along. Torque and therefore motion is produced from the system’s natural tendency to minimize the reluctance (air gap) between the energized electromagnet and the ferromagnetic surface. The electromagnets inside the robot are individually controlled to manage the sphere's position and rotational speed.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}, when an individual electromagnet is powered, it generates a magnetic field, which magnetizes the ferromagnetic surface, resulting in an attractive force downward. The force creates a torque, rotating the ball forward. All necessary electronics, power supplies, sensors, and control devices are contained within the body of the robot. This inverted approach allows us to apply magnetic circuit theory, develop power electronic systems, and implement advanced control algorithms in the context of a challenging and unconventional engineering problem. Concepts drawn from traditional reluctance motors aided in our design, however we did not see any projects similar to this during our research.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Kris_Visuals/Ball_torque_image2.png}
    \caption[Generating torque on the sphere]{The electromagnets around the ball create an attractive force to the ferromagnetic surface, resulting in a rotation about its center.}
    \label{fig:Sphere_Torque_Generation}
\end{figure}

To reduce project risk and aid in early-stage design, we focused our initial efforts on developing a simplified physical prototype: a disk that rotates on a fixed test stand above a ferromagnetic plate. This allowed us to focus on validating magnet designs, build and test the driving electronics, and prototype control software in a more manageable, two-dimensional setup. For more information on last year’s prototype, see the Project 2507 report~\cite{2507_report}.

Building on our previous work, this year we focused on system design, manufacturing, integration, and control. A magnet design was chosen and twenty cores and coils were manufactured, along with twenty identical current control circuits. These isolated electromagnet subsystems were then integrated into a spherical shell geometry, along with the intertial measurement unit (IMU), microcontroller, and batteries. A control architecture was developed to use the IMU data to determine the sphere's orientation and activate the appropriate electromagnets to maintain motion. Finally, we tested the system's performance and identified key areas for improvement in future iterations.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{System Level Overview}

Our goal was to develop a sphere with electromagnets positioned around its surface, each with its own dedicated driving circuit which can control the current in the electromagnet. Every driving circuit receives power from LiPo batteries and communicates with a microcontroller. The microcontroller also receives data from an IMU, which it uses to determine the current orientation of the sphere. Based on this information, the microcontroller sends commands to the driving circuits to activate the appropriate electromagnets to maintain motion.

By breaking the system down into these components, we were able to design and test each one in isolation. This modular approach allowed us to develop the electromagnets independently, while creating and refining the driving circuit in parallel.


\subsection{Electromagnet Theory and Core Design}
The operation of the electromagnets relies on fundamental principles of magnetic circuit theory. When current flows through the coil of an electromagnet, it produces a magnetic field that magnetizes both its ferromagnetic core and the adjacent ferromagnetic plate. This magnetization leads to the formation of two oppositely polarized magnetic regions, resulting in a strong attractive force.


The strength of this force is directly related to the magnetic flux ($\Phi$) flowing through the system. Magnetic flux is driven by magnetomotive force (MMF), analogous to voltage in electrical circuits, and opposed by magnetic reluctance ($\mathcal{R}$), which plays a role similar to electrical resistance. To decrease the reluctance and increase the flux, we minimize the air gap between the electromagnet and the ferromagnetic plate, and use a core material with high permeability. Following detailed testing and analysis outlined in our previous report~\cite{2507_report}, we designed the magnets as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2026_magnet_design}. Notably, the three-pronged design allows for a stronger magnetic field when it is further from the plate due to the outer ``wings'', and the central ``pole'' ensures that there is a single equilibrium position as the magnet approaches the plate. This helps with stability and control. For an in-depth discussion of the design process, see section 2.2.2 of~\cite{2507_report}.



\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/example_sim.png}
    \caption[Simulation of magnetic flux]{ANSYS simulation of the trident core geometry. The vector field demonstrates how magnetic flux is concentrated through the central pole and outer return paths to minimize reluctance across the air gap.}\label{fig:2026_magnet_design}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Optimization of Coil Winding Count}
Selecting the ideal wire loop count ($N$) requires balancing magnetic strength against the rotational inertia added by the wire's mass. Because the coil's diameter expands with each loop, inertia scales non-linearly. To maximize the robot's angular acceleration, we optimized the strength-to-inertia ratio subject to a constant thermal limit ($I^2R$). 

Our mathematical model revealed an optimal configuration at $N=202$ loops.

\subsubsection{Manufacturing}\label{sec: mag manufacturing}
To build each of the twenty electromagnets, we first waterjet cut the cores from ferromagnetic plates, and then wound copper coils using a winding jig and custom mounts and sleeves. The mass ($\approx 520 g.$) and resistance ($\approx 1 \Omega$) of each magnet was measured and recorded to ensure consistency.

\subsection{Magnet Driver Electronics}

\subsubsection{High Level Architecture}
The attractive force produced by a given electromagnet is directly related to the current flowing through it, so independent control of the current in each electromagnet is necessary to achieve intelligent force modulation and control over the motion of the ball. 

Electrically, the electromagnets can be modelled as a series combination of a resistance $(R)$ and an inductance $(L)$. When a voltage is applied across an electromagnet, the current through it rises with an RL exponential characteristic and when the voltage is removed, the current falls with a similar characteristic. By rapidly switching the applied voltage with a MOSFET, the current can be shaped to achieve a desired average value, forming the basis of our current control strategy. Figure~\ref{fig:exampleCurrent} illustrates an example electromagnet current waveform with switching driven by a PWM signal.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/exampleElectromagnetCurrent.png}
    \caption[Example waveforms of electromagnet current due to MOSFET switching]{Illustrative example of the current through an electromagnet as it is modulated through the switching of an applied voltage. When the switch is closed, the current rises with an RL exponential characteristic, and when the switch is opened, the current falls with an RL exponential characteristic. The average current can be modulated by changing the duty cycle of the PWM signal, and the current ripple can be reduced by increasing the PWM switching frequency. The ripple in this example is exaggerated for illustrative purposes, but in practice with switching at frequencies in the 10's of kHz, ripple can be reduced to less than a few percent of the average current.}
    \label{fig:exampleCurrent}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:Schematic} shows the high level architecture used to implement this control strategy. The switching of current through an electromagnet can give rise to large voltage spikes due to inductor kickback if not handled properly. A flyback diode is used to provide a path for the electromagnet current to discharge safely when the switch is opened, and to also avoid abrupt drops in current which would otherwise degrade controllability. The instantaneous current through the electromagnet is monitored using a shunt resistor and differential amplifier, providing feedback for closed-loop control of the current. Based on this current measurement and the desired current, an ESP32 modulates the duty cycle of the MOSFET gate PWM signal to achieve the target current.

\subsubsection*{Switching Stage}
A gate driver (U2) is used to drive the gate of the MOSFET, providing the necessary voltage and current to charge and discharge the gate capacitance quickly, reducing switching losses in the MOSFET. This fast switching allows for higher PWM frequencies, which reduces current ripple, while maintaining tolerable heat dissipation. On turn off events, there is some finite time that the flyback diode takes to conduct and allow the current to discharge. For very fast switching speeds, this can lead to a large drain-source voltage overshoot that can damage the MOSFET. To mitigate this, we limit the switching speed using gate resistors which set the gate charging current. Figure~\ref{fig:gateResComparison}, shows the effect of increasing the gate resistance from 1 $\Omega$ to 13.3 $\Omega$, which is used in the final design (R1, R2). The effect is to bring the voltage overshoot down from the near the MOSFET's maximum drain-source voltage rating of 30 V to less than 4 V overshoot from supply.

\subsubsection*{Current Sensing}
The shunt resistor and differential amplifier are selected to provide optimal accuracy and resolution. A 5 m$\Omega$ shunt (R3) was selected to keep power loss small. Care was taken to select a shunt resistor with low parasitic inductance to minimize di/dt noise in the current measurement. A TI INA180 current sense amplifer (U4) was selected having a gain of 50 V/V giving a measureable current range of 0-13.2 A with its 3.3 V output range. An RC lowpass filter (R6, C4) with 5 kHz cuttoff is used on the output to attenuate high frequency switching noise before the signal is given to the ADC.

\subsubsection*{Power}
Each current driver requires two voltage supply levels to support its operation. The electromagnets must be supplied with high enough voltage to deliver currents up to 10A (based on magnet testing and determined magnet force requirements) through a ${\sim}1\,\Omega$ load (electromagnet series resistance). Additionally a higher supply voltage will increase the rate at which current can rise and fall through the electromagnets, improving responsiveness. To balance battery size and performance, a 14.7 V rail was determined to be sufficient. 

A 3.3 V supply is also required to power the current sense amplifier. This is supplied by the ESP32's internal voltage regulator, to ensure proper referenceing between the amplifier and the microcontroller. In this driver architecture, the gate drive is supplied with the same 14.7 V supply as the electromagnets. When the electromagnets pull current, stray inductance between the magnet terminal and the battery leads cause the supply voltage to sag. If the supply sags too low, the gate driver may enter a lock-out mode, preventing it from driving the MOSFET. A sag in supply voltage also degrades the performance of the current driver since the electromagnet is being driven with a lower voltage. To solve this issue, a large 100 $\mu$F electrolytic capacitor (C2) is placed across the supply rails on the current driver board to provide a local charge reservoir for the current driver to source current from. A small 68 $nF$ (C3) ceramic capacitor is placed in parallel to the electrolytic to filter out high frequency noise on the supply.

\subsubsection{Orientation Tracking}
We need to know the orientation of the ball to know where each electromagnet is relative to the origin of the ball. The magnet location information allows us to determine which magnets need to be activated. 

There are a couple of popular methods for rotation tracking; two common approaches include Euler angles and quaternions, outlined in Figure \ref{fig:angle_tracking_options_quaternion} and \ref{fig:angle_tracking_options_euler}. We chose to utilize quaternions since it does not suffer from gimbal lock and it is more computationally efficient to compute new magnet locations (see \ref{eqn:magnet_rotation}) than the Euler angle approach.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \begin{minipage}{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/euler-angle.png}
        \caption[Euler angle visualization]{Euler angles is a common representation of rotation that uses three angles to represent the orientation of an object.}
            \label{fig:angle_tracking_options_euler}
    \end{minipage} 
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/quaternion-basics.png}
        \caption[Quaternion description]{A quaternion is a mathematical object similar to a vector of size 4 that can represent the orientation of an object in 3D space.}
        \label{fig:angle_tracking_options_quaternion}
    \end{minipage}

\end{figure}

An inertial measurement unit (IMU) is used to determine the rotation quaternion $q$. Using the Madgwick sensor fusion algorithm which combines the data from the accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer, we get a quaternion representing the device's state \cite{Madgwick_2010}. You can learn more about our selection of the IMU in \ref{sec:IMU_selection}.

Our selected IMU provides us with the current orientation "game vector" ($q$) at 100 Hz. The game vector is sent over $I^2C$. Critically, the quaternion allows us to map the coordinates of the magnets in the ball’s reference frame to the world reference frame. The arbitrary rotation of a vector $\vec{v}$ in a body with a rotational state $q$ can be calculated using the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \vec{v}_{\text{rotated}} = q \vec{v} q^{-1}
    \label{eqn:magnet_rotation}
\end{equation}

By utilizing the above formula and the known locations of the magnets in the ball's reference frame, the location of the magnets in the world reference frame is given by the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{V}_{\text{magnet, world}} &= \begin{bmatrix}
            \vec{v}_{1,\text{world}} \\
            \vdots \\
            \vec{v}_{20,\text{world}}
        \end{bmatrix}, \\
        \vec{v}_{i,\text{world}} &= q\,\vec{v}_{i, \text{ball}}\,q^{-1}, \quad i=1,\ldots,20
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eqn:magnet_world_location}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Magnet Selection}
\label{sec:magnet_selection}
To control the rolling motion of the sphere, the system must continuously generate a desired force vector in the global reference frame ($\vec{F}_{\text{world}}$). The user's joystick input dictates a desired two-dimensional velocity. A Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller compares this input against the sphere's current estimated velocity to calculate the kinematic error, $\vec{e}(t) = \vec{v}_{\text{desired}} - \vec{v}_{\text{current}}$. 

By minimizing this velocity error, the controller outputs the required global corrective force:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{F}_{\text{world}} = K_p \vec{e}(t) + K_i \int_{0}^{t} \vec{e}(\tau) d\tau + K_d \frac{d\vec{e}(t)}{dt}
\end{equation}

For each magnet, the algorithm evaluates its angle from the steel plate to find its expected torque from a lookup table based on data from the test stand (see Figure \ref{fig:torque_model}). You will note that the experimental and model curves have a slight misalignment. In practice, we found that prioritizing the lower angles led to better stability in the system. This is because a 5° orientation error was common. Therefore, by prioritizing the lower angles, it ensured that the magnets were strong enough.

To find the optimal combination of magnets to match the desired force, the system evaluates both individual candidates and pairs by minimizing a cost function. This function balances the target force error against an electrical current ($I_j$) penalty ($\lambda$):
\begin{equation}
    \text{Score} = \left\| \vec{F}_{\text{body}} - \sum_{j} (\vec{v}_j I_j) \right\| + \lambda \sum_{j} I_j
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/torque_model.png}
    \caption[Torque model]{Our model of the torque generated by a single magnet at different angles from the steel plate.}
    \label{fig:torque_model}
\end{figure}

The combination (single or pair) that yields the lowest score is selected for activation. Finally, all commanded currents are capped at the current limit. Capping the magnets individually rather than scaling all selected magnets down by the same ratio ensures strength is as high as possible at the cost of changing the angle of the resulting force vector. This tradeoff was made to prioritize responsiveness and agility over precision in the direction of the force vector.


\subsubsection{Magnet Current Control}
\label{sec:magnet_current_control}
Given a magnet and a current set point, we use a simple PI controller to select the appropriate duty cycle for the current. Since we are often required to control the current through multiple magnets simultaneously, we run a sequence of PI controllers in series. The series operation prevents overloading the SPI and I2C communication lines with reads and writes. Although the approach doubles the latency of the control loop, it does not negatively impact the macro control properties.

\subsubsection{Active State}
\label{sec:active}
In the active state, our ESP runs two separate processes, one on each core of the ESP. One core is responsible for receiving and sending data to the dashboard. The other core is responsible for the orientation tracking, magnet selection, and magnet current control. 

The two cores communicate through a globally shared object that contains the current state of the system. All shared memory between cores is protected by mutexes to prevent race conditions between cores.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{Diagrams and Images/core-1-loop.png}
    \caption[Active state diagram]{A diagram outlining the control flow in core 1 loop during the active state.}
    \label{fig:Active_State_Diagram}
\end{figure}

The control loop is run on core 1 with the watchdog turned off to allow us to run our fast loop at $3.33 \text{KHz}$. Every 10 milliseconds, the algorithm checks for new orientation data from the IMU. The magnet selection algorithm runs using the new locations and the most recent joystick command. After two magnets and their current targets are updated, the logic returns to the fast current loop, which is then run with the updated current targets. 

The communication loop on core 0 is responsible for receiving data from the dashboard and sending data back to the dashboard. The outbound data is sent at a rate of $10 \text{Hz}$ and contains the following state variables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current orientation of the ball (quaternion)
    \item Current target for each magnet
    \item Current (and timestamp) through each magnet
\end{itemize}
The inbound data from the dashboard uses a polling approach. When a new direction command is received from the dashboard, the communication loop updates the global state with the new command.

\subsubsection{Unit Cells}

The magnet leads, power wires, and signal connections were soldered directly to the PCB to minimize the number of connectors and potential points of failure. \subsubsection{Icosahedral Geometry and Split Plane Design}
Maintaining spherical symmetry in our design was critical. Rotational inertia, available torque generation, and control complexity are simplified when the system behaves the same in all orientations. From our previous work, and magnet design testing, we found that the magnets were only effective when they were rotated to within 45 degrees of the steel plate normal. These requirements led us to an icosahedral geometry which has twenty faces that are equally spaced around the surface, with 42 degree angles between face centers. This allows us to place one magnet at the center of each face. We designed the sphere to be split into two hemispheres along an edge of the icosahedron, ensuring that no magnets are located on the split plane. This design choice allows for access to the internal components without the need to remove any magnets. See figure~\ref{fig:split_plane} for an image of the split plane design and how the two halves of the sphere combine.

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Integration}
To connect the current driver boards to the microcontroller and batteries, four PCBs were designed and built, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:integration}. The two power distribution boards provide the battery power to the twenty current driver boards, while the signal distribution board takes the microcontroller’s control signals and routes them to the current driver boards. The main microcontroller board has three eight-channel analog to digital converters (ADCs) which read the current sense amplifier outputs and multiplex the signals to send to the microcontroller. Additionally, two sixteen-channel PWM drivers control the gate drivers, with ten magnets per driver. The ADCs use SPI protocol and scan all the input signals, while the PWM drivers used the I2C protocol to communicate with the microcontroller and send individual PWM signals to each magnet. The microcontroller board is connected to an IMU board using I2C. Each half of the sphere contains ten magnets, and a power distribution board. The ESP control board connects to a smaller signal distribution board in the second half to connect the two halves while using only one ESP.

\section{Conclusion}
The primary objective of this project was to develop a spherical robot, capable of moving using the forces generated by internal electromagnets. By successfully integrating twenty individually controlled electromagnets, with a battery supply, IMU and microcontroller, we have proven that internal electromagnetic propulsion is a viable method for achieving motion along a ferromagnetic surface. The successful implementation of the state machine algorithm demonstrates that the system can perform the complex, real-time orientation calculations required to activate the correct electromagnets for sustained movement.

While this prototype successfully validates the core mechanical, electrical, and software architecture, the current design faces performance bottlenecks primarily related to weak torque generation. This weakness stems from several distinct operational and design limitations: 1. LiPo batteries experience voltage drop during use and struggle to consistently deliver the 20 amps of instantaneous current required for optimal performance. While the batteries are rated to provide our desired current maximums, previous usage and old age is likely impacting the performance. 2.\ the current magnet activation algorithm relies on a simple, greedy approach which selects the two magnets delivering the most force in the desired direction. This fails to properly handle the ball's rotational inertia and does not optimize for energy efficiency or advantageous future orientations. 3.\ the weight-to-magnetic-force ratio of the current magnet materials significantly increases the system's polar moment of inertia, mechanically restricting acceleration. 4. Design-wise, utilizing only 20 magnets limits our angular resolution, leaving large gaps of approximately $30^\circ$ between vertical axes. This often forces the robot to travel in suboptimal directions when no appropriately aligned magnets are available.

\section{Recommendations}
Our final design shows a promising path forward for a spherical robot propelled by electromagnets. To address the bottlenecks identified in the conclusion and significantly enhance performance, we recommend the following actionable developments for future iterations or capstone projects:

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{1. Integrate Higher Capacity Batteries}\\
Source and install new batteries with a higher overall capacity and faster instantaneous discharge capabilities to reliably sustain 20 amps of current for a longer duration.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{2. Implement Advanced Magnet Activation Algorithms}\\
Upgrade the magnet selection logic to handle complex motion planning. Specific avenues to explore include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A cost function optimized to place the ball in future orientations that maximize the number of usable magnets.
    \item A cost function optimized for the most efficient use of electrical current.
    \item An improved inertia correction model that dynamically accounts for the ball's current momentum.
    \item A reinforcement learning algorithm that allows the system to learn the optimal magnet selection strategy.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{3. Increase the Number of Magnets}\\
Scale the internal design to incorporate more electromagnets. This will improve angular resolution, eliminate large dead zones, and ensure stronger torque generation in arbitrary directions. Referencing Figure~\ref{fig:torque_model}, decreasing the minium angle between two adjacent magnets from 42 degrees, will
increase the minimum torque generated by the system and increase the acceleration of the ball. We recommend exploring the use of an icosadodecahedron geometry for the electromagnet layout in the next iteration. This increases the number of electromagnets from 20 to 32, and decreases the minimum angle between adjacent electromagnets from 42 degrees to 37 degrees.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{4. Utilize Improved Magnet Materials}\\
Transition electromagnet cores to magnetic materials with superior magnetic properties, i.e. Silicon Steel. This will maximize the system's magnetic strength.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{5. Explore Sheetless Propulsion Methods}\\
Investigate entirely new design families that eliminate the need for a ferrous operating surface. One recommended approach is integrating an outer spherical shell filled with ferromagnetic fluid and applying magnetic fields to manipulate the fluid and move the ball's center of mass.

\section{IMU Selection}
\label{sec:IMU_selection}
There were several IMU options we considered for this project. We selected the FSM300, a 9 degree of freedom IMU with an integrated chip that preforms the Madgwick sensor fusion on device. 

Our IMU performance was analyzed using a stable drift test. In this test, we placed the IMU on a stable surface and recorded the output quaternion over 5 minutes.
\begin{equation}
    \text{Drift} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| q_i - q_{\text{initial}} \|
\end{equation}
The FSM300 had a $10^5$ times lower drift than the BN09X, a cheaper alternative.  



\section{Calibration State}
\label{sec:calibration}
The connection between the direction of the joystick and the local axis of the ball is challenging to connect. Our calibration step is designed to help align the pair of coordinate systems, and is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The magnet with the largest force vector is selected and turned on at $12 \text{amps}$ for 1 second.
    \item The user draws the joystick in the direction of travel of the ball and send that direction to the ball.
    \item The ball sets the yaw offset between the magnet and the joystick direction. The offset is then applied to all future joystick commands.
\end{enumerate}
With this implementation, the user can calibrate at any point in time when the drift affects performance on the UI.
